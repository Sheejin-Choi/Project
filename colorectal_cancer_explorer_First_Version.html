Overall CRC rates have declined ~1% annually since the 1980s due to colonoscopy screening. However, early-onset CRC (under 50) has increased ~2% per year since the mid-1990s — a public health paradox demanding attention.

Risk factors span modifiable lifestyle factors, hereditary syndromes, and environmental exposures. Understanding relative risk helps target screening and preventive interventions to highest-risk populations.

High Risk · Hereditary
Lynch Syndrome (HNPCC)
Autosomal dominant; MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2 mutations. Lifetime CRC risk 40–80%. Accounts for ~3% of all CRC.
High Risk · Hereditary
Familial Adenomatous Polyposis (FAP)
APC gene mutation. Hundreds to thousands of polyps. Nearly 100% lifetime risk without colectomy.
High Risk · Disease History
Inflammatory Bowel Disease
Ulcerative colitis and Crohn's colitis increase CRC risk 2–8× depending on extent and duration of disease.
Moderate Risk · Lifestyle
Obesity & Physical Inactivity
BMI ≥30 associated with RR ~1.3–1.5. Sedentary lifestyle adds independent risk; especially for colon cancer.
Moderate Risk · Diet
Red & Processed Meat
IARC Group 1 carcinogen (processed meat). Each 50g/day processed meat ↑ risk ~18%. N-nitroso compounds, heme iron mechanisms.
Moderate Risk · Lifestyle
Alcohol & Tobacco
Heavy alcohol (≥4 drinks/day) RR ~1.5. Smoking increases risk after 30–40 year latency; particularly rectal cancer.
Moderate Risk · Metabolic
Type 2 Diabetes
Hyperinsulinemia and insulin-like growth factors promote adenoma and CRC development; RR ~1.3–1.5.
Emerging · Microbiome
Gut Microbiome Dysbiosis
Fusobacterium nucleatum enriched in CRC tumors. Altered microbial metabolites (bile acids, SCFAs) implicated in carcinogenesis.
Emerging · Early-Onset
Antibiotic Use & Western Diet
Childhood antibiotic exposure and ultra-processed food consumption hypothesized as drivers of rising early-onset CRC rates.
Sources: ACS Guidelines 2024; IARC Monographs Vol. 114; Sung et al., Gastroenterology 2021; Brenner et al., Lancet 2014.

Screening is the single most effective intervention for reducing CRC mortality — it can prevent cancer by removing precancerous polyps. USPSTF now recommends beginning at age 45 for average-risk adults.

% Adults Up-to-Date with CRC Screening (2021, NHIS)

Colonoscopy
Every 10 years · Sensitivity ~95% (large polyps)
Gold standard. Both diagnostic and therapeutic — polyps removed during procedure. Requires bowel prep and sedation. Low adherence but high efficacy per test.
FIT (Fecal Immunochemical Test)
Annual · Sensitivity ~79% for CRC
Non-invasive stool test detecting blood. High adherence, no bowel prep. Requires annual compliance and colonoscopy follow-up if positive.
Cologuard (mt-sDNA)
Every 1–3 years · Sensitivity ~92% for CRC
Stool DNA test detecting cancer-associated mutations + hemoglobin. Higher sensitivity than FIT but lower specificity (higher false positives).
CT Colonography
Every 5 years · Sensitivity ~96% (≥10mm polyps)
Virtual colonoscopy using CT imaging. No sedation required. Requires bowel prep. Cannot remove polyps — follow-up colonoscopy needed.

Key Policy Milestones
Screening Guidelines Timeline
1997
USPSTF first recommended CRC screening for average-risk adults beginning at age 50.
2008
Colonoscopy formally endorsed alongside stool-based tests. Medicare coverage expanded.
2016
Cologuard FDA-approved as the first stool DNA test. Covered by Medicare for average-risk adults.
2021
USPSTF lowered screening age to 45 for average-risk adults — a major policy shift in response to rising early-onset CRC rates.
2023–Present
Equity-focused initiatives to close the gap in screening uptake among Black Americans, Hispanic populations, and uninsured adults. Navigator programs expanding.
